# From Instructor-Led to Self-Paced

## Project

**Course:** Coaching Skills for Managers  
**Project type:** Instructor-led training to self-paced asynchronous eLearning conversion  
**Instructional designer and prototype developer:** Cici Dong  
**Audience:** New and mid-level managers  
**Original format:** Two-hour virtual instructor-led workshop  
**Redesigned format:** Approximately 30-minute responsive self-paced module  

All source branding, company names, and internal references are anonymized.

---

## Overview

Coaching Skills for Managers was originally delivered as a live, facilitator-led corporate workshop. It introduced managers to three central areas:

- Coaching versus directing
- OSCAR for feedback conversations
- Listening, powerful questions, and GROW

The redesign preserves the substance of those frameworks but changes how managers encounter and practice them.

The goal was not to put a workshop online. It was to transform a two-hour presentation into a concise learning experience that managers could complete on demand, practice through realistic decisions, and revisit when real conversations happened.

---

## Challenge

The instructor-led format presented three central limitations.

### Could not scale

A two-hour session had to be delivered again for every cohort, new manager, and time zone.

### Inconsistent delivery

The learner experience varied with the facilitator, cohort energy, and available session time.

### Passive participation

Managers watched frameworks being explained but had limited opportunities to make decisions, practice conversations, or revisit the material afterward.

The redesign was therefore a learning-experience transformation, not merely a change in delivery format.

---

## Learner analysis

The target learners are new and mid-level managers or team leads. Many were promoted because they performed well in their functions but received little formal people-management training.

Their work context includes:

- Back-to-back meetings
- Fragmented attention
- Short one-to-one conversations
- Immediate performance pressures
- Limited time for formal training

They tend to default to directing because it feels faster:

> “Here is exactly what you should do.”

They are motivated by team performance, stronger ownership, better decisions, and retaining good employees—not by completing an L&D requirement.

### Key learner insight

The central objection was not:

> “I do not understand coaching.”

It was:

> “I do not have time to coach.”

### Design implication

The module is organized around **coaching in minutes**:

- Short, single-focus sections
- Resumable progress
- Everyday management situations
- Five-to-ten-minute conversation examples
- Reusable workplace job aids

---

## Business analysis

### Performance gap

Managers frequently solve problems by directing employees instead of helping them think through decisions. This can keep teams dependent on the manager and limit employee ownership and capability.

### Business goal

Managers use small coaching behaviors in daily work, including:

- Structuring feedback through OSCAR
- Asking open and purposeful questions
- Listening before proposing a solution
- Running a short GROW conversation
- Knowing when clear direction is more appropriate than coaching

### Organizational value

Managers who coach can develop stronger problem-solving and ownership across their teams. A standardized asynchronous course can also provide more consistent access across cohorts and time zones.

---

## Why asynchronous

The asynchronous version responds directly to the limitations of the original format.

| Instructor-led workshop | Asynchronous redesign |
|---|---|
| Two-hour scheduled session | Approximately 30 minutes |
| Repeated for each cohort | Available on demand |
| Presenter-controlled pace | Learner-controlled and resumable |
| Dense explanatory slides | Progressive, single-focus screens |
| Limited practice | Guided examples and decision activities |
| Facilitator-dependent experience | Standardized learning experience |
| Few post-session resources | Downloadable job aids |

The redesigned course is shorter because presentation time and repetition were removed—not because the learning substance was removed.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of the module, a manager will be able to:

1. Distinguish coaching from directing and identify when each is appropriate.
2. Apply OSCAR to structure a feedback conversation.
3. Formulate powerful open questions using clarifying, challenging, commitment-gaining, and listening question types.
4. Use GROW to run a short coaching conversation.
5. Select an appropriate coaching approach for common management situations.

## Instructional strategy

The project follows the Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, and Evaluate logic of ADDIE.

The conversion also uses several core principles:

- Break dense material into short, single-focus segments.
- Reveal information progressively.
- Demonstrate a skill before asking learners to perform it.
- Replace passive presentation with decisions and feedback.
- Use realistic situations rather than decontextualized recall.
- Treat incorrect selections as opportunities to teach.
- Provide job aids that support workplace transfer.

---

## Module blueprint

### Introduction

Reframe coaching as a different way of communicating rather than an additional task. Establish the approximately 30-minute commitment.

### Part 1 — What is coaching?

- Coaching versus directing
- What coaching is and is not
- Manager and coach as two roles
- Coaching-or-directing knowledge check

### Part 2 — How to coach

#### Feedback through OSCAR

- Introduce feedback as a conversation
- Reveal OSCAR progressively
- Demonstrate a full worked conversation
- Practice through a multi-turn scenario
- Download the OSCAR card

#### Listening and powerful questions

- Connect listening and questioning
- Introduce four active-listening habits
- Introduce four families of questions
- Match questions to their purposes
- Explore and download the question library

### Part 3 — Coaching in real life

- Introduce GROW
- Complete a guided GROW conversation
- Choose an approach for realistic scenarios
- Clarify when coaching should not be used

### Wrap-up

- Reinforce three central takeaways
- Download OSCAR, questioning, and GROW cards
- Encourage use during the next real conversation

## Design decision 1: Progressive OSCAR demonstration

### Before

The original workshop presented the OSCAR framework through dense explanatory slides.

### Design move

The asynchronous course reveals one component at a time:

- Observation
- Specific
- Consequences
- Actions
- Results

The interface shows instructional guidance on one side and a worked manager-to-employee conversation on the other. The example accumulates line by line as the learner progresses.

### Rationale

This approach:

- Manages cognitive load
- Connects an abstract framework to an observable conversation
- Supports backward and forward review
- Demonstrates performance before practice

### Intended learning effect

Learners see how a full coaching conversation develops instead of memorizing isolated labels.

---

## Design decision 2: Branching OSCAR practice

### Situation

Sam is a capable employee whose weekly status report has arrived late and without required revenue figures three times.

The manager needs to address the pattern without turning the conversation into blame or reprimand.

### Practice structure

The learner makes four decisions:

1. Open with an observation.
2. Surface the consequences.
3. Invite Sam to identify an action.
4. Connect the action to a future result.

### Feedback design

Every selection receives specific explanatory feedback.

Examples of weak-choice feedback identify:

- Generalization
- Judgment
- Defensive “why” wording
- Telling instead of asking
- Premature closure
- Missing commitment

Weak choices return the learner to the decision rather than ending the activity.

### Rationale

The activity assesses application, not acronym recall. It rehearses the conversational judgment managers need at work.

---

## Design decision 3: Performance support

The original live session ended when the workshop ended.

The redesigned experience provides three downloadable cards:

- OSCAR
- Powerful-question types
- GROW

Each card condenses the framework into a practical workplace reference.

### Rationale

The cards:

- Reduce dependence on memory
- Support use at the moment of need
- Extend learning beyond course completion
- Connect training to workplace performance

## Proposed evaluation

### Level 1: Reaction

Measure:

- Relevance to managers’ daily work
- Confidence using the coaching frameworks
- Perception of the 30-minute format
- Usefulness of the scenarios and job aids

### Level 2: Learning

Review:

- Decisions made in OSCAR and GROW activities
- Ability to distinguish coaching from directing
- Ability to classify and formulate powerful questions
- Quality of responses in the final scenarios

### Level 3: Behavior

Follow up after several weeks to examine:

- Use of coaching questions in one-to-ones
- Use of OSCAR or GROW during real conversations
- Manager reflection or supervisor observation
- Continued use of downloadable job aids

### Level 4: Results

Where organizational data permits, explore:

- Team ownership
- Reduced dependency on managers
- Quality of development conversations
- Relevant engagement indicators
- Relevant retention indicators

These are proposed evaluation measures, not completed results.

## Reflection

My UX background helped me organize complex information, prototype interactions, and design a clear interface.

This project required me to go further: begin with a performance need, define observable learning outcomes, align practice with those outcomes, and write feedback that actively teaches.

An eLearning conversion is not successful because the original content fits on a screen. It is successful when the new format provides a better way for learners to understand, practice, and use the skill.
